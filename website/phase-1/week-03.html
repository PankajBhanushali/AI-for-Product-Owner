Core Concepts

Why Ethics Matter in AI

AI systems make decisions affecting millions of people. From loan approval to medical diagnosis to content recommendation to hiring—biased or unethical AI amplifies existing injustices at scale.

Core Principle: Just because we can build something doesn't mean we should. Product owners have responsibility to deploy AI responsibly.

Regulatory Pressure: EU AI Act, California AI transparency laws, industry scrutiny. Ethics is increasingly a legal & business requirement.

📌 The Big 4 AI Ethics Challenges

1. Bias & Fairness

The Problem: Training data reflects historical biases. Feed historical data into a model → model learns historical discrimination.

Real Example: Amazon's recruiting tool was trained on 10 years of hiring data (which favored men in tech). The model learned to discriminate against women applicants. Amazon scrapped it after backlash.

Other Examples:

  • Facial recognition works worse on darker skin tones
  • Loan approval models deny credit to minorities at higher rates
  • Healthcare algorithms underestimate illness in Black patients

For Product Owners: Audit your training data for biases. Test models on diverse groups. Monitor for disparate impact (different outcomes for different groups).

2. Privacy & Data Protection

The Problem: AI requires data; data contains sensitive user information. Training data can be extracted. Models can be reverse-engineered.

Real Example: LLMs trained on internet text include personal information:

  • Home addresses scraped from websites
  • Phone numbers from public databases
  • Email addresses and credit card numbers
  • Medical records, legal documents, private emails

The Risk: Users' private information is embedded in model weights. Could potentially be extracted via adversarial attacks.

For Product Owners: Implement data anonymization. Be transparent about data use. Comply with GDPR, CCPA, HIPAA. Minimize data collection to what's necessary.

3. Transparency & Explainability

The Problem: Deep learning models are "black boxes"—hard to explain why they made a decision. Users have no recourse.

Real Example: A bank's AI rejects your loan application. When you ask why, the bank says "the AI decided." No explanation. No way to appeal.

Why it matters: For high-stakes decisions (credit, jobs, parole, medical treatment), people deserve explanations.

For Product Owners: For high-stakes decisions, require explainability. Use interpretable models when possible. Be honest with users about AI limitations. Provide appeals mechanisms.

4. Accountability & Responsibility

The Problem: When AI causes harm, who is responsible? The engineer? Managers? The CEO? The AI itself?

Real Example: Autonomous vehicle hits a pedestrian. Is blame on the engineer who coded the decision logic, the company that deployed it, the vehicle manufacturer, or the AI itself?

Why it matters: Without clear accountability, companies can hide behind "the AI made the decision." Victims have no recourse.

For Product Owners: Set clear governance. Assign responsibility to humans (not "the AI"). Have human oversight for critical decisions. Track decisions for audit. Establish accountability chains.

📖 Case Study: COMPAS Recidivism Algorithm

What Happened: US courts used a commercial AI tool called COMPAS to predict if criminals would reoffend. Used to determine bail amounts and parole.

The Finding: Investigative journalists (ProPublica) discovered the model was 2x more likely to falsely flag Black defendants as future criminals compared to white defendants.

Root Cause: Trained on historical crime data, which reflects systemic bias in arrest patterns (Black people arrested more frequently due to policing disparities, not higher criminality).

Impact: Thousands of wrongful detention decisions, destroyed lives, legal challenges, public controversy.

Lessons:

  • Biased data → biased model, no matter how sophisticated
  • AI at scale amplifies existing injustices
  • Technical solutions alone aren't enough (need governance)
  • Audit before deployment. Transparency is critical.

🛡️ Building Ethical AI: Your Framework

7-Step Checklist for Responsible AI:

  1. Audit Your Data
    • Understand where data comes from
    • Check for biases (are some groups underrepresented?)
    • Test model fairness across demographics
  2. Diverse Teams
    • Include perspectives from affected communities
    • Hire ethicists, social scientists, not just engineers
    • Red-team your own project (find problems before launch)
  3. Transparency
    • Tell users how the AI works
    • Disclose its limitations clearly
    • Publish impact assessments
  4. Human Oversight
    • For critical decisions, always have human review
    • Make humans the decision-maker, AI the advisor
    • Never fully automate high-stakes decisions
  5. Monitoring
    • Track outcomes over time (bias can drift)
    • Monitor for unintended consequences
    • Be ready to unplug if it goes wrong
  6. User Redress
    • Provide ways for users to challenge or appeal AI decisions
    • Have human review for appeals
    • Make reversal/correction easy
  7. Privacy First
    • Minimize data collection (only what's necessary)
    • Anonymize where possible
    • Implement differential privacy techniques

⚠️ Red Flags: When NOT to Use AI

Skip AI if any of these are true:

  • ❌ You don't have diverse representation in your training data
  • ❌ You can't explain why the model makes decisions
  • ❌ Errors would cause significant harm to users
  • ❌ You can't monitor outcomes over time
  • ❌ Users have no way to appeal or override the decision
  • ❌ You don't have budget for ongoing monitoring
  • ❌ You're just using AI because it's trendy (not solving a real problem)

🤝 The Human-in-the-Loop Principle

The best AI systems don't replace humans—they augment them. Three models:

AI as Advisor

Human makes the final decision. AI provides recommendations. (Example: medical diagnosis assistance)

AI as Filter

AI handles routine tasks. Humans handle exceptions. (Example: spam email detection with manual review queue)

AI as Explainer

AI discovers patterns. Humans interpret and act. (Example: anomaly detection in financial transactions)

Key Insight: The more critical the decision, the more you need human involvement. AI excels at augmenting human judgment, not replacing it.

✏️ Your Exercise This Week

Ethical Analysis of an AI Product

Pick an AI product you use regularly (Netflix recommendations, LinkedIn suggestions, ChatGPT, etc.) and evaluate it ethically:

  1. Data Collection: What data does it collect from you? Is it transparent about it?
  2. Bias Potential: Could the data or model contain biases? Who might be harmed?
  3. Impact of Errors: What happens if the model gets it wrong? What's the real cost?
  4. Transparency: Is the decision transparent to you? Do you understand why it recommended X?
  5. Appeal Mechanism: Can you override it? Can you appeal if it's wrong?
  6. Fairness: Does it treat different user groups equally?
  7. Redesign: How would you redesign it to be more ethical?

💡 Document this: You'll present your analysis in Phase 2 strategy work.
