📚 Week 1: AI Literacy & Fundamentals

Core Concepts

What is Artificial Intelligence?

AI is the simulation of human intelligence processes by computer systems. It encompasses three main categories:

  • Narrow AI (weak AI): Systems designed for specific tasks (all current AI systems)
  • General AI (strong AI): Hypothetical systems with human-level intelligence across domains
  • Super AI (ASI): Theoretical AI surpassing human intelligence (not yet realized)

Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning vs. AI

Understanding the relationship between these terms:

AI (Broadest)
→ Any technique that enables computers to mimic human behavior
Machine Learning (Subset of AI)
→ Systems that learn and improve from experience without explicit programming
Deep Learning (Subset of ML)
→ Neural networks with multiple layers that learn from large amounts of data

Types of Machine Learning

  • Supervised Learning: Learning from labeled data (email spam detection)
  • Unsupervised Learning: Finding patterns in unlabeled data (customer segmentation)
  • Reinforcement Learning: Learning through rewards and penalties (game AI)

📖 Practical Example: Email Spam Detection

The Problem: Gmail receives 200+ million spam emails per day

The AI Solution:

  1. Train: Feed the model millions of labeled emails (spam/not spam)
  2. Learn: Model identifies patterns (sender reputation, keywords, links)
  3. Predict: New email is analyzed and classified in milliseconds
  4. Improve: User feedback retrains the model

Key Takeaway: AI excels at pattern recognition in large datasets—it won't "understand" emails like humans, but it spots patterns humans would miss.

🎓 Key Insights for Product Owners

  • AI is NOT magical—it's pattern matching at scale
  • AI needs data, lots of it. Quality matters more than quantity
  • AI works best for well-defined, specific problems
  • AI cannot replace human judgment; it augments it

💬 Week 2: Large Language Models & Generative AI

What are Large Language Models?

LLMs are neural networks trained on massive amounts of text data (books, articles, websites) to predict the next word in a sequence. This simple task, repeated billions of times, creates emergent intelligence.

Examples: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama

How Do LLMs Work? (Simplified)

  1. Tokenization: Text is broken into tokens (words/subwords)
  2. Embedding: Each token becomes a vector (array of numbers)
  3. Transformers: Attention mechanism weights which tokens matter most
  4. Prediction: Output the most likely next token
  5. Repetition: Use predicted token as input for next prediction

Result: Coherent text that seems to understand context and meaning

Capabilities: What LLMs Are Good At

  • ✅ Text generation and creative writing
  • ✅ Question answering and information retrieval
  • ✅ Code generation and debugging
  • ✅ Summarization and abstraction
  • ✅ Translation and linguistic tasks
  • ✅ Role-playing and conversation

Limitations: What LLMs Cannot Do

  • ❌ Real-time information (they have a knowledge cutoff)
  • ❌ Mathematical precision consistently
  • ❌ Access to external systems or websites
  • ❌ True reasoning (it's pattern matching, not thinking)
  • ❌ Guaranteed accuracy (they can "hallucinate" false facts)
  • ❌ Understanding images, audio, or multimodal data naturally

📖 Practical Example: Customer Support Chatbot

The Scenario: E-commerce company handles 10,000 customer support tickets monthly

The AI Solution:

  1. Train LLM on past support conversations (best practices)
  2. Add retrieval system to access company knowledge base
  3. Deploy chatbot to handle 70% of common issues instantly
  4. Route complex issues to human agents

Impact: 24/7 support, faster response times, agents focus on complex issues

Challenge: Hallucinations could give wrong product information—need human review

🎯 Prompting Basics: How to Talk to LLMs

Good Prompt Strategy:

  • Be specific: "Summarize this in 2 bullets" vs "tell me about this"
  • Give context: "You are a technical writer for engineers..."
  • Provide examples: Show the style/format you want
  • Set constraints: Word limits, format requirements, tone

Example:

❌ "Write me a product description"

✅ "Write a 2-sentence product description for a AI-powered task manager. Target audience: busy professionals. Tone: professional but friendly. Focus on time-saving benefits."

  3. Ask it to access a website

⚖️ Week 3: Ethics & Responsible AI

Why Ethics Matter in AI

AI systems make decisions affecting millions of people. From loan approval to medical diagnosis to content recommendation, biased or unethical AI can cause real harm.

Key Principle: Just because we can build something doesn't mean we should. Product owners have responsibility to deploy AI responsibly.

📌 The Big 4 AI Ethics Challenges

1. Bias & Fairness

Problem: Training data reflects historical biases

Example: Amazon's recruiting tool discriminated against women because it was trained on historical hiring data (which favored men)

For Product Owners: Audit your data. Test models on diverse groups. Monitor for disparate impact.

2. Privacy & Data Protection

Problem: AI requires data; data contains sensitive user information

Example: LLMs trained on internet text include personal information (addresses, phone numbers, emails)

For Product Owners: Implement data anonymization. Be transparent about data use. Comply with GDPR, CCPA, etc.

3. Transparency & Explainability

Problem: Deep learning models are "black boxes"—hard to explain decisions

Example: A bank's AI rejects a loan but can't explain why. Applicant has no recourse.

For Product Owners: For high-stakes decisions, require explainability. Be honest about AI limitations to users.

4. Accountability & Responsibility

Problem: When AI causes harm, who is responsible?

Example: Autonomous vehicle hits a pedestrian. Is blame on the engineer, company, or AI itself?

For Product Owners: Set clear governance. Have human oversight for critical decisions. Track decisions for audit.

📖 Real-World Case Study: COMPAS Recidivism Algorithm

What Happened: US courts used COMPAS AI to predict if criminals would reoffend and set bail amounts

The Problem: Investigative journalists found the model was 2x more likely to falsely flag Black defendants as future criminals

Root Cause: Trained on historical crime data reflecting systemic bias in arrest patterns

Impact: Thousands of wrongful detention decisions, destroyed lives, legal challenges

Lesson: Biased AI at scale amplifies existing injustices. Technical solutions aren't enough—you need ethical frameworks.

🛡️ Building Ethical AI: A Framework for Product Owners

  1. Audit Your Data: Understand biases. Test for fairness across demographics.
  2. Diverse Teams: Include perspectives from affected communities in design.
  3. Transparency: Tell users how the AI works and its limitations.
  4. Human Oversight: For critical decisions, always have human review.
  5. Monitoring: Track outcomes over time. Be ready to unplug if it goes wrong.
  6. User Redress: Provide ways for users to challenge or appeal AI decisions.
  7. Privacy First: Minimize data collection. Anonymize where possible.

✏️ Exercise 3: Ethical Analysis

Evaluate an AI Product:

Pick an AI product you use (Netflix, LinkedIn recommendations, etc.)

  1. What data does it collect from you?
  2. Could that data contain biases?
  3. Who could be harmed if the model is wrong?
  4. Is the decision transparent to you?
  5. Can you appeal or override it?
  6. How would you redesign it more ethically?
